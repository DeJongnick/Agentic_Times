Why Sunak's five promises won't be much use in an election - but are better than nothing

Here is a question from below the line that I‚Äôll answer up here because it‚Äôs a peg for making more points about Rishi Sunak‚Äôs five promises.

The short answer is no, for at least two reasons.

First, Rishi Sunak would be mad to hold a snap election in the spring. The Politico poll of polls currently gives Labour a 22-point lead over the Conservatives. He won‚Äôt call an election in those circumstances, and these five promises are not enough to shift the numbers. That because ‚Ä¶

Second, these aren‚Äôt promises that have been weaponised for an election. To work well as election pledges, promises have to be 1) popular, 2) specific and memorable, and 3) hard for the other side to match.

The five Sunak promises will probably all be popular with voters. If you poll people, they are unlikely to say they are against reducing inflation, growing the economy, cutting NHS waiting lists etc.

But mostly they are not specific or memorable (unlike, say, the five pledges Labour made in 1997, which in some respects were very modest ‚Äì ‚Äúwe will cut NHS waiting lists by treating an extra 100,000 patients as a first step‚Äù ‚Äì but which were focused). And the first four at least are ones that Labour would happily endorse, so they have zero electoral wedge potential.

The fact that three of the pledges relate to the economy suggest Sunak is still thinking as a chancellor, not a prime minister. And he may be overestimating how grateful people will be for inflation being brought under control. If energy prices stop going up for the next 12 months, energy price inflation will be down to zero. But people will still be paying much higher prices than they used to.

The only promise that does read like a Lynton Crosby election trap for Labour is the final one, on small boats. But Sunak would not say what it actually meant. (See 3.59pm.)

That said, it would be a mistake to think that the promises are pointless. At the weekend Policy Exchange, a Tory thinktank, published a report on what the government should do in 2023. It included an extraordinary poll finding.

When asked what the government has done well on since 2019, over 30% of those who responded said ‚Äònothing‚Äô ‚Äì a figure that rises further when other, similar, answers are included. Covid and vaccines; Brexit; Ukraine; and furlough were positive achievements cited by respondents.

The report included the results as a word cloud.

These promises are a response to that ‚Äúnothing‚Äù. Tory candidates will at least have an answer when they campaign, and get asked what the government is doing, and by the time of the next election they should be able to list five promises made and five delivered (more or less). It won‚Äôt be a winning ploy, but it will be start, and an improvement on the status quo. This is how Robert Hutton from the Critic sums it up.

PROMISE ONE: ‚ÄúWe will halve inflation this year to ease the cost of living and give people financial security.‚Äù

Reality: According to the OBR‚Äôs latest forecasts, CPI inflation in the last quarter of this year is already set to fall to 3.8%, nearly two thirds lower than in the last quarter of 2022 (11.1%).

The prime minister‚Äôs pledge is therefore likely to be less ambitious than existing official forecasts.

PROMISE TWO: ‚ÄúWe will grow the economy, creating better-paid jobs and opportunity right across the country.‚Äù

Reality: According to the latest OECD forecasts, the UK is one of the only advanced economies to not grow this year ‚Äì so we could hardly do worse than we were. In contrast, our competitor economies like France, Italy and the US are expected to grow sustainably over this year and next.

PROMISE THREE: ‚ÄúWe will make sure our national debt is falling so that we can secure the future of public services.‚Äù

Reality: Targeting lower public sector net debt is an existing government fiscal target that the OBR already expected it to meet. At the end of the forecast period (2027/28), the OBR still expects debt to be 30% higher as a percentage of GDP than when they came to power.

PROMISE FOUR: ‚ÄúNHS waiting lists will fall and people will get the care they need more quickly.‚Äù

Reality: According to the respected Institute for Fiscal Studies, waiting lists were already set to fall in the second half of 2023. Treatment numbers tend to increase after the winter and the number of people on waiting lists is predicted to peak in the middle of this year and then fall providing the NHS is able to treat patients at usual volumes

PROMISE FIVE: ‚ÄúWe will pass new laws to stop small boats, making sure that if you come to this country illegally, you are detained and swiftly removed.‚Äù

We‚Äôll find out later whether it succeeds in the respect. But if this is the plan, then there are at least three reasons why this might be a problem. Sometimes too much vision can be a bad thing.

1) Political vision can be over-rated. Sunak, like other prime ministers in the past, has been criticised for focusing wholly on day-to-day topics dominating the headlines, and not setting out ‚Äì or even having ‚Äì a long-term vision of what he wants to achieve in politics. But when prime ministers do embark on ‚Äúthe vision thing‚Äù they get criticised (normally by the same people) for ignoring what matters to voters. Sunak will almost certainly get more questions from the media this afternoon on strikes and the state of the NHS than on education, or maths. 2) Voters don‚Äôt always find ‚Äúpolitical vision‚Äù convinicing. In the trail for his speech, Sunak says:

Every opportunity I‚Äôve had in life began with the education I was so fortunate to receive. And it‚Äôs the single most important reason why I came into politics: to give every child the highest possible standard of education.

3) Politicians who do set out a personal vision often fail to achieve it. He said his priorities were ‚Äúeducation, education, education‚Äù and schools improved considerably during his premiership.
